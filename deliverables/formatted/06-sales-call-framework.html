Sales Call Framework

This document covers what happens AFTER the discovery call — when you know their problem and you're presenting your solution and pricing. If you haven't done the discovery call yet, use the Discovery Call Script first.

The Difference Between Discovery and Sales

Discovery Call Sales Call
You're listening You're presenting
Goal: understand their problem Goal: show them how you solve it
They talk 70%+ of the time You talk 50-60% of the time
No pricing discussion Pricing is discussed
Outcome: "Let me put together a proposal" Outcome: "Let's get started"

Sometimes these happen in the same conversation. Sometimes you do a discovery call first and come back with a proposal a few days later. Both are fine.

Before the Sales Conversation

Prepare your proposal. Keep it simple — 1 page max.

Proposal Template

For: [Client Name / Business Name]
Date: [Date]
Prepared by: [Your Name]

The Problem:
[In their own words — use the exact language they used during discovery]
Example: "You mentioned that your team spends 3-4 hours per day manually following up with leads, and that you're losing potential customers because responses take too long."

The Solution:
[Outcome-focused, not tool-focused]
Example: "An automated lead follow-up system that responds to every new inquiry within 60 seconds, 24/7 — so your team can focus on closing deals instead of chasing leads."

What's Included:
- [Deliverable 1: e.g., Automated lead response system]
- [Deliverable 2: e.g., Integration with your existing CRM]
- [Deliverable 3: e.g., Video walkthrough + written documentation]
- [Deliverable 4: e.g., 2 weeks of testing and optimization]

Timeline: [X] weeks

Investment: $[amount]

Payment Terms: 50% upfront, 50% upon completion
(or: 100% upfront with a 10% discount)

How to Present Pricing

Rule 1: Sell the Outcome First, Then Price

Before you say any number, make sure they clearly understand what they're getting. Not the automation — the RESULT.

"Based on what you shared, here's what this would look like for your business..."
- "Every lead gets a response within 60 seconds"
- "Your team saves 3-4 hours per day"
- "You stop losing deals to competitors who respond faster"

THEN present the price.

Rule 2: Frame Price as ROI

Don't just say "$2,500." Frame it:

"The investment for this is $2,500. Based on what you told me — you're losing roughly [X] leads per month because of slow follow-up. Even if this system captures just 2-3 extra deals per month at your average deal size, you'd make back the investment in the first month."

Rule 3: Use Anchoring

Before presenting your price, reference what the problem is costing them:

"You mentioned your team spends 15-20 hours per week on this manually. At $25/hour, that's $2,000-$2,500 per month in labor costs. The one-time investment to automate this is $2,500 — so it pays for itself in the first month and saves you money every month after."

Rule 4: Present Payment Options (Not Price Options)

Don't give 3 different pricing tiers. Give ONE price with flexible payment terms:

  • "The investment is $3,000. We can do 50% upfront and 50% upon completion, or if you'd prefer to pay in full, I can offer a 10% discount."

During the Sales Conversation

Step 1: Recap the Problem (2 minutes)

"Last time we spoke, you mentioned [problem]. Has anything changed since then?"

This confirms the problem is still real and reminds them why they're talking to you.

Step 2: Present the Solution (5 minutes)

Walk through your proposal. Focus on outcomes:
- "Here's what your business looks like after we set this up..."
- "Every lead gets a response within [time]"
- "Your team gets [X hours] back per week"
- "You have full documentation so your team can manage it"

Step 3: Present the Price (2 minutes)

Use the ROI framing from above. Be confident. Don't apologize for your price.

Say the number and stop talking. Let them respond.

Step 4: Handle Objections (5-10 minutes)

"That's more than I expected"
→ "I understand. Let me ask — what would it be worth to you to [outcome]? If this saves your team 15 hours per week and captures even 2-3 more deals per month, what's that worth over the next 12 months?"

"I need to think about it"
→ "Totally understand. What specifically would you need to think through? I want to make sure I've answered all your questions."

"Can you do it cheaper?"
→ "This is the price for the full solution. If budget is a concern, we could start with [smaller scope] at a lower price and add on later. But honestly, doing the full solution gives you the best result."

"I need to talk to my partner/team"
→ "Of course. Would it help if I put together a one-page summary you can share? Or would it make sense for all of us to hop on a quick 15-minute call so they can ask questions directly?"

"What if it doesn't work?"
→ "We test everything thoroughly before handoff — end-to-end testing, edge cases, multiple runs. I don't hand off anything that doesn't work. And I include 2 weeks of support after delivery to make sure everything runs smoothly."

"I've been burned before"
→ "I hear that a lot. Here's what makes this different: [mention your testimonials if you have them]. I also give you weekly progress updates throughout the project so you always know exactly where things stand. And you get full documentation so you're never locked in — you own everything."

Step 5: Close

If they're ready:
→ "Great, let me send over the invoice and we can get started this week."

If they need time:
→ "No problem. I'll send this proposal over to you today. When would be a good time to reconnect — [specific day]?"

Always set a specific follow-up date. "I'll follow up next week" is too vague.

After the Sales Conversation

If they said yes:
- Send invoice immediately (same day)
- Do NOT start work until payment is received (no payment = no work)
- Schedule the onboarding call (see Client Onboarding Checklist)
- Send a confirmation message: "Thanks [name]! I'm excited to get started. I'll send over the invoice today and once that's sorted, we'll schedule our onboarding call to kick things off."

If they said "let me think":
- Send the proposal within 24 hours
- Set a follow-up reminder for the agreed date
- Follow up on that date: "Hey [name], just checking in on the proposal I sent over. Do you have any questions I can help with?"
- If no response after 2 follow-ups, one final message: "Hey [name], just wanted to check in one last time. If the timing isn't right, totally understand — the offer stands whenever you're ready."

If they said no:
- Thank them for their time
- Ask: "Is there anyone you know who might be dealing with a similar challenge?"
- Keep them on your list — they might come back later

Retainer Upsell (for Delivered Clients)

After you've delivered a project successfully, this is how you pitch ongoing maintenance:

"Hey [name], now that [project] is live and running, I wanted to mention something. A lot of my clients find it valuable to have ongoing support — making sure the system runs smoothly, optimizing it based on real data, and handling any issues that come up. I offer a monthly maintenance retainer for $[amount]/month, which covers support, updates, and optimization. Would that be useful for you?"

Retainer pricing formula: 20% of the implementation fee
- $2,500 project → $500/month
- $5,000 project → $1,000/month

What the retainer includes:
- System monitoring and maintenance
- Bug fixes and adjustments
- Monthly optimization based on performance data
- Priority support (response within 24 hours)

Key Principles

  1. Never discount your price to close a deal. Reduce scope instead. "We could start with [smaller piece] at $[lower amount] and add the rest later."
  2. The client is buying an outcome, not hours of your time. Don't price by the hour. Price by the value of the result.
  3. Confidence sells. If you seem unsure about your price, they'll feel unsure about paying it.
  4. No payment, no work. Period. Get payment confirmed before you start building anything.
  5. The upsell happens naturally. Deliver great work → offer a business audit → find more problems → solve them → repeat.
